# BFS-08: Altruistic Landscape — Building for the Human, Not the Feature

**Date:** 2026-05-13  
**Scope:** Human-centered, altruistic, and long-term view of the nursing exam-practice project.  
**Research Phase:** BFS — breadth-first landscape of human needs and ethical impact  
**Author:** Kimi Code CLI  

## 1. Core Question

If the user's mother were sitting next to us, what would she actually need? Not what is technically impressive, but what is **kind, useful, and respectful**?

## 2. Who Is the Learner?

A woman in Telangana, likely:
- Aged 30–50 (preparing for government staff-nurse recruitment).
- Trained as a nurse (GNM/B.Sc) but possibly years out of formal study.
- Balancing family responsibilities.
- Unsure of the exact exam format because notifications change.
- Possibly anxious about English-medium testing.
- Using a smartphone more than a laptop.

This is not a "user." This is a mother trying to secure a stable government job. The stakes are economic and social, not just academic.

## 3. Altruistic Design Principles

### A. Dignity first
- Do not treat her like a child. Avoid cartoonish gamification, childish mascots, or patronising language.
- Use clean, calm, adult-appropriate design.
- Celebrate effort and improvement, not just scores.

### B. Reduce anxiety, not add to it
- Show progress clearly.
- Explain *why* an answer is right or wrong in plain language.
- Allow unlimited retries. The goal is learning, not ranking.

### C. Work with real life
- She may have 15 minutes while cooking or waiting for a bus. Support short sessions.
- Offline PDF export lets her study without data.
- Save progress automatically so interruptions don't punish her.

### D. Language as a bridge, not a barrier
- The exam is English-only, but comprehension is easier with Telugu support.
- Provide Telugu UI labels and optional term glossaries.
- Never make her feel "less than" for needing Telugu help.

### E. Trust through transparency
- Every medical fact should show its source.
- A "Report question" button gives her agency.
- Clear disclaimer: practice aid, not a guaranteed predictor.

## 4. Landscape of Existing Solutions

| Solution | Strength | Weakness | Altruistic Gap |
|----------|----------|----------|----------------|
| Paid coaching apps (Testbook, Adda247) | Large question banks, mock tests | Subscription cost, generic content, can be overwhelming | Expensive for low-income families; not personalised |
| YouTube lecture videos | Free, vernacular | Passive, not adaptive, hard to track progress | No active recall or spaced practice |
| WhatsApp study groups | Peer support | Unverified answers, distraction, no structure | Risk of misinformation |
| Textbooks | Authoritative | Static, no analytics, no timed practice | Don't adapt to weak areas |
| Our `/nursing` module | Personalised, free, transparent, offline-capable | Small initial question bank | Needs careful content verification |

## 5. Long-Term Altruistic Vision

If this module works for one mother, it can be extended for:
- Other Telangana nursing aspirants.
- Other state staff-nurse exams (AP, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu).
- Other healthcare worker recruitments (MPHA, lab technicians, pharmacists).
- A community-maintained, verified nursing MCQ bank.

The altruistic output is not just an app; it is a **reusable, trustworthy public-learning asset**.

## 6. Risk: Building the Wrong Thing

The biggest altruistic failure is building something she does not use because:
- It is too complex.
- It requires English fluency she does not have.
- It needs reliable internet she does not have.
- It feels like a game for children.

Mitigation: involve her in early feedback; design for her context, not ours.

## 7. First-Principles Reframing

> The goal is not to build a nursing app. The goal is to help a mother pass an exam that changes her family's future.

Every technical decision must trace back to that goal.
